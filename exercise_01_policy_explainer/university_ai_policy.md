# Artificial intelligence use policy
**Institution:** Halcyon University
**Applies to:** All students, faculty, staff, and researchers
**Effective date:** September 1, 2026
**Policy owner:** Office of Academic Affairs
**Review cycle:** Annual — next review August 2027
**Status:** Approved

---

## Purpose

This policy establishes principles and guidelines for the responsible use of artificial intelligence tools at Halcyon University. It is intended to support academic integrity, protect individual rights, promote critical thinking, and enable the University community to benefit from AI while managing its risks.

This policy does not prohibit AI use. It establishes a framework within which AI may be used appropriately, transparently, and ethically.

---

## Scope

This policy applies to:

- All academic coursework, assessments, dissertations, and research conducted under the auspices of Halcyon University
- All staff use of AI tools in administrative, operational, and communications functions
- Use of University-provided AI tools and personally sourced AI tools alike
- On-campus and remote activity

It does not apply to personal use of AI tools entirely unrelated to University activities.

---

## Definitions

**Artificial intelligence (AI) tool:** Any software application that uses machine learning, large language models, generative models, or similar technologies to produce text, images, code, data analysis, or other outputs. This includes but is not limited to: ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, Midjourney, and discipline-specific AI research tools.

**AI-assisted work:** Any work in which an AI tool contributed materially to the content, structure, analysis, or expression of the output, regardless of the extent of subsequent human editing.

**Generative AI:** A subset of AI tools capable of producing original text, images, audio, code, or other media in response to prompts.

**Academic assessments:** Examinations, essays, problem sets, lab reports, presentations, dissertations, theses, and any other work submitted for academic credit or evaluation.

---

## Core principles

### 1. Transparency
AI use must be disclosed. Members of the University community are expected to be honest about when and how AI tools have contributed to their work.

### 2. Academic integrity
AI tools may not be used to misrepresent a student's own understanding, effort, or authorship unless explicitly permitted by the course instructor or assessment guidelines.

### 3. Critical engagement
AI outputs must be critically evaluated. Members of the University community are responsible for the accuracy, quality, and integrity of any work they submit or publish, regardless of whether AI contributed to it.

### 4. Equity and access
Policy implementation must account for variation in access to AI tools. No student or staff member should be disadvantaged by inability to access AI tools where their use is expected or encouraged.

### 5. Data protection
Personal data, confidential University data, and unpublished research must not be entered into external AI tools without appropriate authorisation and an understanding of the tool's data handling practices.

## Academic use — students

### Permitted uses (unless otherwise stated by instructor)

- Using AI tools for brainstorming, ideation, and exploring a topic
- Using AI to generate outlines or structural suggestions that the student then develops independently
- Using AI tools for grammar checking, proofreading, and language support
- Using AI to assist students writing in a second language, provided the intellectual content remains the student's own
- Using AI tools for coding assistance where the student understands and can explain the code produced
- Using AI to generate practice questions or study materials for self-directed learning

### Uses requiring explicit instructor permission

- Using AI to generate text that is incorporated directly or with light editing into a submitted assessment
- Using AI to conduct research summarisation or literature review in lieu of engaging with primary sources
- Using AI to produce data visualisations, images, or multimedia submitted as assessed work
- Using AI translation tools for assessed written work

### Prohibited uses (in all circumstances)

- Submitting AI-generated content as entirely one's own work without disclosure
- Using AI to complete take-home examinations or timed assessments unless explicitly permitted
- Using AI to impersonate another person or fabricate sources, citations, or data
- Using AI tools to gain an unfair advantage over other students in competitive assessments
- Entering another person's unpublished work, personal data, or confidential information into an AI tool without their consent

### Disclosure requirements

Where AI has been used in assessed work, students must include an AI use statement at the end of their submission. The statement should specify:

1. Which tool(s) were used
2. The nature of the AI's contribution (e.g. brainstorming, grammar checking, code generation)
3. How the student verified, edited, or built upon the AI output

Failure to disclose AI use where it has occurred will be treated as a breach of academic integrity policy and may result in disciplinary action.

## Academic use — faculty and instructors

### Assessment design

Instructors are encouraged to design assessments that require demonstrated understanding that AI alone cannot replicate — oral examinations, in-class work, process documentation, and reflective components all remain robust against AI substitution.

Instructors must clearly communicate their AI use policy to students at the start of each course, in the course syllabus. In the absence of explicit guidance, the University's default policy applies.

### Use of AI in teaching

Instructors may use AI tools to develop teaching materials, generate examples, or assist in course design. Where AI-generated content is used in teaching, instructors should model transparency by acknowledging it.

### AI in assessment marking

AI tools must not be used as the sole or primary means of marking or grading student work. AI may be used to assist in administrative aspects of assessment (e.g. formatting feedback, identifying submission issues) but human academic judgement must govern all grading decisions.

## Research use

### Permitted uses

- Using AI tools to assist with literature search and synthesis, provided findings are independently verified
- Using AI for coding, data processing, or statistical analysis, with appropriate documentation of methods
- Using AI to assist with drafting, editing, or translating research manuscripts, provided the intellectual content and conclusions are the researcher's own
- Using AI image or data generation tools where disclosed in methodology

### Disclosure requirements

Research outputs — including journal articles, conference papers, theses, and grant applications — must disclose AI tool use in accordance with the standards of the relevant journal, funder, or discipline body. Where no external standard exists, researchers should include a statement in the methods or acknowledgements section.

AI tools may not be listed as authors or co-authors on research outputs. Authorship requires the ability to take accountability for the work, which AI tools cannot do.

### Data governance

Researchers must not input unpublished data, participant data, proprietary datasets, or confidential research materials into external AI tools without:

- Confirming the tool's data retention and training policies
- Obtaining ethics board approval where participant data is involved
- Checking compliance with any funder or institutional data agreements

Where uncertainty exists, contact the Research Data Management team before proceeding.

---

## Staff and administrative use

Staff may use AI tools to assist with routine administrative tasks including drafting communications, summarising documents, generating reports, and supporting operational functions, subject to the following:

- No personal data (staff or student) may be entered into external AI tools without verifying the tool's data processing agreement
- AI-generated content used in official University communications must be reviewed and approved by a human before sending
- Staff are responsible for the accuracy of any AI-assisted output used in their work
- Use of AI tools for HR decisions — including recruitment, performance review, or disciplinary processes — requires approval from HR and must comply with applicable equality legislation

---

## Prohibited uses — all members of the University community

Regardless of role, the following uses are prohibited:

- Using AI to generate, distribute, or amplify disinformation or deliberately misleading content
- Using AI to create or distribute content that harasses, discriminates against, or harms any individual or group
- Using AI to circumvent University security systems, access controls, or data protections
- Using AI to process University data on tools not approved by IT Services where such data is classified as confidential or restricted
- Using AI to impersonate University officials, staff, or students

---

## Enforcement and consequences

### Students
Violations of this policy in an academic context will be handled under the University's Academic Integrity Procedure. Outcomes may include: a written warning, requirement to resubmit work, grade penalty, course failure, or in serious cases, suspension or expulsion.

### Staff and researchers
Violations will be addressed under the relevant HR or research misconduct procedures. Outcomes will depend on severity and context.

### Reporting
Suspected violations may be reported to the Academic Integrity Office (students) or the relevant line manager or HR Business Partner (staff). Reports may be made anonymously via the University's ethics reporting channel.
